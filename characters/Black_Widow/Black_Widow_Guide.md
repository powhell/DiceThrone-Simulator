## Anatomie des Dés (AABBBC + 123456)

Chaque dé de Black Widow possède **exactement** cette répartition de faces :

| Face | Symbole     | Numéro | Quantité (par dé) |
|------|-------------|--------|-------------------|
| 1    | ESPIONAGE   | 1      | 1                 |
| 2    | ESPIONAGE   | 2      | 1                 |
| 3    | BATONS      | 3      | 1                 |
| 4    | BATONS      | 4      | 1                 |
| 5    | BATONS      | 5      | 1                 |
| 6    | WIDOW       | 6      | 1                 |

**Total par dé : 2A – 3B – 1C**  

**Notation standard (utilisée dans les guides Dice Throne) :**  

**2A 3B 1C**  

- **A** = Espionage (2 faces, numéros bas 1-2)  
- **B** = Batons (3 faces, numéros centraux 3-4-5) → « triple face » mentionnée sur BGG  
- **C** = Widow (1 face, numéro haut 6)

**Straights (utilisent les numéros 1-6, peu importe le symbole) :**  

- **Small Straight** = 4 numéros consécutifs (ex. 1-2-3-4, 2-3-4-5, 3-4-5-6)  
- **Large Straight** = 5 numéros consécutifs (1-2-3-4-5 ou 2-3-4-5-6)

Tu lances **5 dés** par tour. Les attaques se déclenchent selon :  
- Combos de symboles (ex. 3B, 2A+1B)  
- Straights  
- Ou combos spécifiques listés sur le board.

## Section Leaflet (règles officielles)

### COVERT OPS  

**Unique Status Effect** – **Stack limit : 3**

**Utilisation :**  

Tu peux dépenser **1 token une fois par tour** pendant ta **Main Phase** pour :  

- Mettre une **Ability Upgrade** de ta main en jeu.  
- Regarder les **3 cartes du dessus** de ton deck.  
- Si aucune n’est une Ability Upgrade → révéler et chercher dans le deck une Ability Upgrade (montrer à l’adversaire, ajouter à ta main, mélanger).  
- Sinon → remettre dans n’importe quel ordre.

**Important :** Ce token **ne peut ni être transféré ni retiré** par aucun effet.

---

### AGILITY  

**Positive Status Effect** – **Stack limit : 2**

Dépense + lance **1 dé d'attaque** pour éviter **½ dégâts** (arrondi supérieur).  

Fonctionne contre **n’importe quel type de dégâts** (sauf Ultimate) et à **n’importe quel moment**.

---

### TIME BOMB  

**Negative Status Effect** – **Stack limit : 2**

**Désamorçage :** Lancer un **6**.  

**Si tu rates :** avance le token (voir ci-dessous).  

**Placement :**  

- ≥6 Ability Upgrades en jeu → pose sur le côté **0:01**.  
- Sinon → pose sur le côté **0:02**.

**Upkeep Phase (du joueur qui a le token) :** Lance 1 dé d'attaque  
- 1-5 → avance le Time Bomb.  
- 6 → retire le token.

**Quand le Time Bomb est avancé :**  
- S’il est sur le côté **0:02** → retourne-le sur le côté **0:01**.  
- S’il est sur le côté **0:01** → le joueur retire le token et subit **4 dégâts** (source isolée, non défendable).

## Guide de Stratégie BGG Complet (Character Strategy Series: Black Widow par ArgonJedit)

Black Widow is one of the most unique characters in Dice Throne. Not only is she the only character to have her dice anatomy, she also has a completely unique mechanic that changes her relationship to upgrades. It turns one of the most frustrating experiences in the game, drawing an upgrade card, into one of the most rewarding. She can also be quite powerful with some practice, as she develops the strongest offense in the game if built correctly. If the idea of using upgrades in new and exciting ways appeals to you then Black Widow may be the character for you.

**Offense:** At her base level her ORPs are not very damaging. However, she ramps up very quickly. She will upgrade her ORP abilities, which increases the damage they do, and the act of upgrading itself increases her damage due to her passive. Then she has Time Bomb, which also gets more dangerous from upgrading. She has okay damage potential from her defense, which gets quite good after it is upgraded. Finally, she has a couple of unique cards with 3-4 damage potential each. Top that off with 2 abilities on her board that scale in damage based on the number of upgrades in play, and she can become one of the most damaging characters in the game after a couple of turns. Only the ramp up time required stops her from reaching Gold here, but make no mistake, she out damages even Artificer after the first 3 or 4 turns.

**Defense:** Her base defense is only going to mitigate 1 damage on average. However, it will be upgraded very quickly, and with the upgrade the average defense roll, using the reroll to chase Espionage, mitigates over 2 per use. On top of that she has multiple ways to generate Agility, which mitigates another 2 damage on average per use. Add in Elude, Assemble, and Recoil and she has a lot of mitigation options. That said, her defense roll is often going to be used to inflict damage rather than mitigation, either by chasing Time Bomb or direct damage, so she isn’t going to maximize her mitigation potential in most games unless she is willing to give up offense to do it. As such, she usually has average defense.

**Control:** Her control options are limited to just looking at the top cards of her deck and rearranging them, and usually she is disappointed to be doing this as it means her Covert Opps failed. She usually follows this up with another Covert Opps, meaning the rearranging only impacted the 1 turn. She also has a unique card that does basically the same thing. If all her Covert Opps work, she wont use her control at all. When she does use these abilities, they are rarely impactful as she sacrifices the control for economy. She does also have a reroll she can activate on her defense as well, when she hits the threshold. She uses that ability more often, and to better effect.

**Economy:** Speaking of economy, she has a good one. Covert Opps allows her to draw cards out of her deck. Red Room Training II allows her to draw cards anytime she plays upgrades. She can fetch upgrades out of her deck with her Ultimate and Micro-ult. She gains CP with Grapple and Widow's Gauntlets. She can draw upgrades with Cunning. She isn't likely to go though her entire deck, but she will usually draw at least 8 or 10 additional cards. She can also use Covert Opps as CP if she really needs to, by using it to play an upgrade for free.

**Difficulty:** Black Widow’s biggest weakness is her limited ORP options. She only has 6 “attack” roll objectives, and outside of her basic attack the “easiest” one only has a 60% chance of being activated if you chase it. 2 require 4 or more sixes. She will add some alternate attacks quickly with her upgrades, but she can be slow out of the gate. Also, her unique dice anatomy is unforgiving for players chasing her most important ability, Widow’s Gauntlets. While her triple face being in the middle of her dice spread instead of the front does make her basic play much better with her straights, it makes Widow’s Gauntlets play much worse with them than the 2&3 abilities other characters have. Infiltrate doesn’t play nice with her basic or her straights. She has to decide after roll 1 if she is going the straight/basic approach, or going for the riskier Widow’s Gauntlets with Infiltrate fallback. The end result is often an early game with a lot of Infiltrates and Baton Strikes, which makes her early damage output seem lackluster.

In addition, she has to have a game plan for her Covert Opps. Using them the right way, and fetching the right upgrades for the matchup is key. Outside of Red Room Training her priorities for the rest of her Covert Opps will change from matchup to matchup, so she has to know what to grab when against who, which requires a lot of knowledge about every character and matchup.

And finally, she is one of the most CP starved characters in the game. Finding a way to pay for all the upgrades she wants to play can be very difficult. It can sometimes be very hard to overcome the fear of selling a card you might need later, but she absolutely has to flood the board with upgrades. Knowing what cards to sell and keep in each matchup with her is another wrinkle that requires much game knowledge and analysis.

--- Abilities ---

The probability of rolling each ability assumes you are strictly rolling to get that ability from the start, and will be displayed after the ability name as (##%). This does NOT factor in dice manipulating cards or abilities. If the ability has different levels of difficulty, they will be ranked top to bottom.

I’m going to do something a bit unique when discussing her abilities. Normally I give an assessment of when it is worthwhile to play upgrades. However, because she is always going to play every upgrade she gets her hands on, I will instead discuss the priority of each upgrade. By that I mean, when given the opportunity to fetch an upgrade with Covert Opps, Recon, or Widow’s Bite, what upgrades are most important to fetch.

**Baton Strike:** (98%, 88%, 51%) An exactly average basic attack. That said, outside of Samurai you probably activate your basic more than any other character. Both because your unique dice anatomy tends to force you onto it, and also because you always upgrade it.

This upgrade increases the damage of the ability by 1, but also allows you to view your opponent’s hand. Normally, this will be one of the last upgrades you hunt up, but there are 2 circumstances where you may want to hunt for it above some others. The first is when you need a 1 CP cost upgrade you can play this turn to hit a threshold (we will talk thresholds in the strategy section.) The other is when you have some dice mods in hand and are thinking of forcing an Ultimate, Grapple or Widow’s Gauntlets. Looking at your opponent’s had to see if they can stop you will be invaluable in that circumstance.

**Infiltrate:** (72%) This is the easiest ability on her board to activate, but the first time it is used it probably doesn’t deal any damage. It does mitigate about 2 damage on average, and on average will deal 3 damage delayed for 2 turns. That is worse than a basic attack, usually. It is better when used consecutively, as it both increases the damage output, and shortens the delay.

However, this is one of her better upgrades, as it both shortens the delay and lowers the risk of losing the damage on the ability. In addition, it adds Spy Game (27%) which is both a strong, undefendable attack, and one of only 3 options she has for getting more Covert Opps. In many games this is her 3rd upgrade priority. In any game where undefendable damage is a must, this is her easiest way to get it. Sometimes this can even be her 2nd priority, if she doesn’t need the defense upgrade. This should never be lower than her 6th upgrade played, as at 6 upgrades this ability just becomes guaranteed damage.

**Hacked:** (60%) This is almost always stronger than a basic attack at the base level, but really outpaces the basic after the 3rd upgrade hits the board. If not chasing Widow’s Gauntlets this is probably where she wants to be.

The upgrade is a simple damage upgrade. It is low priority, as the 1 damage it provides isn’t worth picking it over other upgrades that give more options. It does only cost 1 CP, meaning sometimes she can play it when she wouldn’t have been able to play any of the others, but Baton Strike is a better 1 CP upgrade. This will usually be one of her last 2 or 3 upgrades in priority. Exceptions are when she is very CP starved, or she can take advantage of weak defenses by spamming the small straight.

**Grapple:** (11%) It gives Agility, does great damage, and provides free CP. After the first few turns this ability is often stronger than your ultimate, baring damage prevention effects from the opponent. Not as easy to hit as Window’s Gauntlets, but very satisfying when you do.

The upgrade makes the CP gain automatic, but let’s be real, at only needing 2 upgrades is pretty much already was on the base ability. It also ups the damage by 1, which is okay. The real value of the upgrade is Recon, (36%) which lets you hunt up other upgrades and put them directly into play. You usually want this ability right about the time you run out of Covert Opps. It will likely be your 4th or 5th upgrade played. That is because if your Covert Opps didn’t get you to your key thresholds you want to have the option to Recon to get there. Also, this ability is often what you dice mod for to end the game, so that extra point of damage doesn’t hurt.

**Red Room Training:** I mean, this is your whole gimmick. Gives you 3 Covert Opps at the start of the game, lets you play upgrades at Roll Phase speed, and gives you the 5 upgrades threshold. Pretty good, but oh boy, the upgrade.

This is your priority 1 upgrade, no matter the matchup. With the Red Room Training II in play whenever you play any other upgrade you also draw a card. Why is that a big deal? Well, card draw wins games. But aside from that, look at it this way. You start the game with 32 cards. You draw 4 on your first turn, now you have 28. Over the first 3 turns, you Covert Opps 3 cards out of your deck. 25. You draw 3. 22. You play Red Room Training, and let’s say 3 other upgrades, allowing you to draw 3 more. 19. In just the first 3 turns you’ve gone through 1/3rd of your deck, and thinned it out to the point where drawing the good stuff is much more likely. Basically, this upgrade doubles the value of all other upgrades you play after it. For that reason you want it in play before you put any others out. One last thing it does for you, you need a lot of CP to play all those upgrades. Drawing cards allows you to sell cards for CP. Play a 2 CP upgrade, draw a card, sell the card for 1 CP, and you basically played the upgrade for half price. Always chase this upgrade first, no matter the matchup.

**Widow’s Gauntlets:** (49%) This is your most important ORP ability. Early game, this is okay damage with much needed CP generation. Late game this is your most damaging attack that doesn’t require 4 sixes. Maxed out this is a 16 damage attack that gives a CP. There is no point in the game you don’t benefit from rolling this ability. It is the best ability to hit on turn 1 outside of the ultimate, and the 2nd best ability to hit on most turns thereafter (Grapple being the best once you have 2 upgrades in play.)

The upgrade is a 1 damage increase (really 2, because of how the ability works.) That is okay. It also adds Covert Mission (87%) which is, meh. It is your only real “fall back” ability on your cards, so it serves a purpose. After 6 upgrades it is actually kind of okay in some matchups. This upgrade is usually either your 4th or 5th priority, depending on if you need Recon or not. You don’t care about the extra 1 damage from it early, but you do want it once you’ve got your bases covered and you start actively hunting this ability.

**Vengeance:** (24%) Your large straight can do some cool stuff. It does decent damage for a large straight when you factor in Time Bomb, and very rarely it will give you a Covert Opps. It also has 2 mitigation on average baked in. Not too shabby.

The upgrade just adds 1 rider die, which ups the damage by less than 1 on average, and slightly increases your chance for Covert Opps. This is a far less reliable way to get Covert Opps than Spy Game, however. The upgrade does also add Subvert (92%) which is an absurdly easy way to get Covert Opps, but does no damage. Usually this upgrade is quite low on your priority list. Subvert letting you get easy Covert Opps is great, but usually Grapple and Infiltrate upgrades are better. While the alternate abilities on those are not nearly as easy to roll, the upgrades to the base ability they give while also providing an alternate means to get new upgrades is usually better. This upgrade is often your last priority, with one exception. If you “miss” on your first couple of Covert Opps and find yourself with no or only 1 upgrade on board with your final Covert Opps, you might consider searching this up to spam Subvert for a couple of turns. You will need to get lucky on the Agility to stay in the match, but you were probably going to lose at that point anyway, so this gives you a chance.

**Sabotage:** Your base defense is pretty good. It will average slightly below 3 true value. Once you get 4 upgrades, the base defense is not really affected. You can reroll a 6 if you don’t want it, or reroll the other two for the 30% shot at a Time Bomb. Sometimes you may want to reroll damage into mitigation or mitigation into damage, but overall the reroll doesn’t add much to the final value of the ability at base level.

The upgrade adds an extra die, which really allows you to get some mileage out of that reroll for the 4 upgrade threshold. If you hit a 6 on the first 4 dice it is usually worth throwing the other 3 back for the 42% shot at a Time Bomb, because if you miss, you still get 3 value from those dice, and the odds you roll more than 1 six on 3 dice is low. I wouldn’t throw the dice back without 1 six, however, as the most likely outcome is that you give up 1 value. In most matchups this is your number 2 priority after Red Room Training. However, against certain characters that never trigger defense rolls if they don’t want (Paladin, Ninja) this becomes your 7th or 8th priority, or maybe doesn’t get played at all.

**Widow’s Bite:** (1%) Your ultimate. Like many characters in the Marvel set, this is more of a setup ability than a finisher. It is better the earlier in the game you roll it, and in some matchups you would actually rather take Grapple than this in the late game. Still, you are never disappointed to roll an ultimate and as long as you have at least 2 upgrades left to play it is good value.

--- Unique Cards ---

Black Widow has 8 upgrades and 6 unique actions. These are common values. The average CP cost for her cards is 1.31, which is on the lower end of CP costs. However, she has very high CP demands, because she draws extra upgrade cards, which average 1.75 in CP cost. Despite her ability to generate extra CP with some of her abilities and her ability to play upgrades for free with Covert Opps, she will still struggle with CP in the first few turns, and will probably need to sell cards. Her CP problems ease later in the game.

**Assemble:** Could be as much as 100% damage mitigation for 1 CP. Averages ½ damage for 1 CP. This is quite a strong card vs. highly damaging characters, nearly as good as a second Not This Time in the deck. Of course, it can also just fail to work. Combos very well with another card on she has. Usually a keep, should be one of the last cards she considers selling. I can't do the little accent mark over the "e" with my keyboard, but just pronounce this like you're French and you'll get it.

**Elude:** This is the card Assemble combos with. That said, this card can only be played 33% of the time an Agility is spent. Sometimes you will only spend 3 or less Agility in an entire game. This effect is very powerful when it works, but it can often be a dead card in your hand. Unless you are spamming Agility as a stall tactic, you have Assemble in your hand, or you are spamming Infiltrate for undefendable damage this is one of the cards you can consider selling.

**Recoil:** Amazing when you hit a 6, which is 30% of the time. If you only hit Batons this is worse than selling the card, and if you only hit the Espionage it is equal to selling the card. If I’m behind I might hold and play this card with Six It or something, but in the early game I probably sell this for the guaranteed CP to get upgrades out quicker so I never find myself in that position.

**Subversion:** Her attack modifier. It is, okay. There are situations where you add 4 damage with this, but most of the time it is 3. 3 damage for 1 CP is still very playable. Don’t spend early CP on this though, save that early CP for upgrades and play this once you have hit your target threshold or you are CP flush.

**Undercover Mission:** With less than 4 upgrades you should never play this card. The 2 extra value from waiting to play this until after you hit 4 upgrades is good, but more importantly if you don’t have 4 upgrades in play yet you need the 2 CP this costs for getting more upgrades out instead. If this is in my hand early I often sell it, but I like to see it late in the game, especially if I’m at 6 or more upgrades.

**Cunning:** Oh Cunning, how I wish I could love thee. On paper, this card is amazing. And if you find yourself in a position to play it, you will love it. Unfortunately, it is very difficult to play this card when you would want to play it. Ideally you want to play this card early to fish out some upgrades to kick start your buildup. But, if you spend 2 CP to fish out upgrades, then what are you going to use to pay for the upgrades you draw? You can of course Covert Opps an upgrade into play, but then that is one less Covert Opps you have to hunt key upgrades and thin your deck. However, there is one situation where this card is a bit of a lifesaver. If you Covert Opps and find a couple of upgrades in the first 3 cards on your deck, using this to clear them and set up your deck to ensure your next Covert Opps works is crazy good. Also, having this turn 1 to make absolutely sure you can find Red Room Training is amazing as well, but you better have some CP coming from somewhere else. Later in the game this card is okay to stack your deck with. Between turn 2 and turn 4, it is hard to play this card efficiently. I never want to sell this card, but I have had to in some situations where I was just not going to have the CP for it AND the upgrades.

--- Strategy ---

Black Widow should come with a flow chart. It would go something like this: Turn 1, draw cards. Did you draw Red Room Training? Yes? Play Red Room Training. No? Use Covert Opps. Did you find an upgrade in the top 3? No? Fish up Red Room Training. Yes? Put the upgrade on top, and repeat these steps next turn. Seriously, Red Room Training II is just so, so important to her game that getting it into play is priority #1, always. After that, she usually wants her defense upgrade, followed by whichever attack she is going to lean on in the match. See the Abilities section for information regarding the priority order for her upgrades.

When it comes to rolling her ORP, again, flow chart time. Did the opening roll contain a lot of 6s? Yes, Grapple or the Ultimate. No? Did the opening roll contain 2 or more Espionage? Yes, chase Widow’s Gauntlets, with Infiltrate as the fallback. No? Chase a straight, with Baton Strike as the fallback. Obviously this is a bit of an oversimplification, but not by much. You pretty much have 2.1 ways to go with her. The 0.1 is for a lot of sixes. The other 2 are chase Widow’s Gauntlets, or chase a straight. These two paths would normally have a lot of overlap on most characters, but because of her unique dice facing it is much harder to transition a WG into a straight or vice versa than it is on most characters. It is easier to hit basic attacks, however, so you do have that.

Sometimes you do want to break from this strategy and actively pursue either Infiltrate or spam straights, however. When opponents have really strong defenses you want to play around then Infiltrate and its alternate attack Spy Game are your best option outside of 4 or more sixes to do that. In those matches you probably want this ability even over Widow’s Gauntlets until you get some upgrades out. In matches where the opponent has particularly weak defense, getting to 3 upgrades and then spamming straights is a safe and reliable way to pile on damage. Chasing straights will sometimes “miss” into Widow’s Gauntlets as well, which is even better. In these two situations Infiltrate or Hacked are usually your 3rd upgrade, unless you don’t need the defense upgrade.

Another key to your strategy is a concept I’m going to call “threshold targeting.” By this, I am referring of course to the different effects that come online at different numbers of upgrades on her board. The thresholds and their effects are, in order: 2 Upgrades – Grapple gives 1 CP when activated. 3 Upgrades – Hacked gains 2 extra damage. 4 Upgrades – Sabotage gains a reroll. 5 Upgrades – All her ORPs do 1 extra damage. 6 Upgrades – Time Bombs start on the 1 second side.

In different matchups some of these thresholds are more important than others. We will ignore the Grapple one, as you will pretty much always hit that. If you are in a match where you are spamming straights then hitting that 3 upgrade threshold is very important. Playing Red Room Training, Sabotage, and Hacked will get you 3 extra damage on your small straights for just 5 CP. Conveniently, you start with 3 Covert Opps to track down these abilities. Sometimes, this is all you need. Repeatedly hitting your opponent for 8 and a Time Bomb turn after turn can win games.

In games where your opponent struggles to do undefendable, targeting 4 upgrades can be very strong. Getting your defense from 3 to more than 4 expected value every turn can be quite punishing over the course of a game, especially if you hit a couple of Time Bombs.

Of course, 5 upgrades is a very nice threshold to target in general, as it is an across the board increase in damage. However, by the time you hit 5 upgrades you can probably only expect 3 to 4 extra damage, because you don’t have more than that many turns left. Also, thanks to using Grapple or Widows Gauntlets, Black Widow very often will do overkill damage, so sometimes the extra damage from 5 upgrades is irrelevant. Still, it is nice to have.

The final target is 6, which is where you want to be for Infiltrate spam. It just so happens that Infiltrate spamming tends to stretch games out, giving you plenty of time to actually get to 6 upgrades. It is a strategy that feeds itself.

How this impacts your game plan is that you need to know which of these targets you NEED to hit for the matchup you are in. Decide right away what threshold you are targeting, because it will impact the priority order of your upgrades. For instance, if you are targeting the 6 upgrade threshold then the Grapple and even Vengeance upgrades are higher priority for you. If you are only targeting the 4 upgrade threshold then getting your main attack upgrades out is more important.

Also, knowing what your threshold is allows you to understand when to make the transition from an economy focused game to a damage focused one. In the economy portion of your game you are likely selling cards and hunting upgrades with your Covert Opps. Once your hit your threshold you can start holding on to those cards and instead start looking for enabling cards like dice mods.

Obviously more upgrades are better, and you want to play all that you draw. 8 upgrades is the dream, but more times than not you won’t get to 8, and you will need to make choices. Knowing what upgrades you want, and how many you NEED is the key to playing Black Widow well in any matchup.

Speaking of upgrades, when is using her special ability to play them during roll phase useful? Actually, not all that often. With Red Room Training in play you tend to want to get your upgrades out quickly so you can draw cards quicker and decide what to do after seeing what you draw. That typically means you are still going to play the upgrades in Main Phase 1. However, there are some circumstances where you will use this ability, such as: You have 2 upgrades in hand, but can only afford to play 1 of them. You can enter your ORP, see if you roll one of them, and then play that upgrade prior to lock in. Also, if you have the defense upgrade, and also something like Helping Hand, you can wait until your opponent’s ORP to see if you want to use your CP on Helping Hand, or if they trigger an undefendable attack before you play the defense upgrade. Another case is if you expect your opponent is eager to mod your rolls, hold your CP until after your ORP is locked in, then play your upgrades. You will not get the benefit of the upgrade on any ability you rolled that turn, but you can still trigger the extra damage from number of upgrades in play on Widow’s Gauntlets, Grapple, or the 5 upgrade threshold. There are other fringe cases where you benefit from this ability, but for the most part you still want to play upgrades Main Phase 1 to get cards into your hand faster and make decisions with the maximum amount of options and information available to you.

To end with I’ll touch briefly on each of her status effects. The best use of Covert Opps is to fish out key upgrades and thin her deck, but don’t be afraid to use it as CP if you already have a couple of key upgrades in hand. For instance, paying to play Red Room Training, then using Covert Opps to put something like Infiltrate into play, and then draw a card to replace it, is 2 CP and a card for 1 token. Pretty good value. Also, if you ever hit 8 upgrades don’t just let Covert Opps sit on your board. It still allows you to rearrange your top 3 cards which is a pretty decent effect in and of itself. Oh, and if you ever use a Covert Opps and see Triple Up, Not This Time, and Twice as Wild (the power 3) on top of your deck turn 1, you still want to shuffle them away and go get your Red Room Training. It is that good. You can’t afford to play that Twice as Wild right now anyway.

Agility, if you have 2, play one against any attack your defense doesn’t completely nerff. If you only have 1, save it for preventing 4 or more damage (7 left after your defense roll.) Agility has an average value of 2 if you play this way.

Time Bomb will blow up on the opponent 70% of the time if they don’t remove it with some other effect. 70% of 4 is just under 3 value, so it is actually quite strong. So strong in fact that it will almost always be the target of any removal they draw. That is okay though, as it allows you to roll more Agility, and get deeper into the game where you have more upgrades and become more dangerous. Once you hit 6 upgrades it has a value of 3.36, and is very hard to remove. It is very strong post 6 upgrades.

**Counter Play:** Hit her hard early. She is a middle turn specialist who takes a few turns of setup but becomes very dangerous in the middle turns (4-6). She can swing for very high damage values in those rounds. If you can rush her down early it can force her to do things like chase Agility tokens instead of Widow’s Gauntlets making her less dangerous in the middle rounds. Also, if she has any hiccups in the first rounds, like “missing” on her first Covert Opps, you can sometimes win the game on pure rushdown.

Save your damage avoidance for late. If you have cards or effects that prevent all or half damage, save them for the late game. Preventing 8 damage at the start of the game is fine, but preventing 14 at the end is better. If the card prevents a specific value of damage, like Not This Time, feel free to play that whenever you get maximum value, but scaling prevention you should hold.

Along the same token, save control effects for Widow’s Gauntlets or Grapple (or the Ultimate).

Black Widow is a middle turn specialist. She tends to require a few turns of setup to get going, but not nearly so many as other “build up” or late game characters. She plays a nice middle of the road game that makes her less vulnerable to rush downs than typical build up characters, but also means she is not quite as dominant her final form, due to having little control over the board state. She is very strong against characters who lean on defense rolls, like Paladin and Cursed Pirate, due to her ability to completely negate them with Infiltrate. She is a bit weak to strong rushdown characters like Pyromancer and Artificer, but not terribly so. She is a bit more vulnerable to characters with great damage avoidance abilities like Ninja and Monk, as they can sit on Smoke Bomb/Evasive and make her Widow’s Gauntlets and Grapples risky propositions. She is also very vulnerable to characters who steal CP like Shadow Thief who can not only make her life hard out of the gate, can completely shut her down in the late turns.

Black Widow is strong against characters who rely on defense rolls, and when allowed to set up by the midgame.  
Black Widow is weak to damage avoidance based characters, and great control characters.
